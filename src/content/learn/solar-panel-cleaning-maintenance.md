## Do Solar Panels Need Cleaning?

Yes. In India's climate — with dust, bird droppings, pollen, and seasonal haze — solar panels accumulate surface soiling that reduces the amount of sunlight reaching the cells. Even a thin layer of dust can measurably reduce output.

The impact varies by location:
- In Lucknow, Delhi, or other north Indian cities with dry winters, dust accumulation can be significant between rains
- In coastal regions, salt mist and humidity may require different maintenance approaches
- In high-pollution industrial zones, soiling rates are highest

Regular cleaning and monitoring help ensure your system performs at or near its rated capacity throughout its 25-year life.

## Recommended Cleaning Schedule

| Location Type | Cleaning Frequency |
|---------------|-------------------|
| Low dust (hills, coastal areas) | Every 4–6 months |
| Typical residential (most Indian cities) | Every 2–3 months |
| High dust or industrial zones | Monthly inspections recommended |

During monsoon season, natural rainfall often handles light dust. Increase cleaning frequency during dry winter months (October–February) in north and central India.

## Safe Cleaning Methods

- **Use clean water** — Tap or filtered water works well for most soiling; avoid hard water that leaves mineral deposits
- **Soft cloth or non-abrasive brush** — A microfibre cloth or soft-bristle brush removes dust without scratching the glass surface
- **Clean during early morning or evening** — Avoid midday heat; sudden cold water on hot glass panels can cause thermal stress
- **Do not use pressure washers directly on panel edges** — Water forced into frame gaps can damage wiring and connections
- **Follow manufacturer cleaning recommendations** — Some panel warranties may have specific maintenance requirements
- **Consider professional services for height access** — Rooftop cleaning carries fall risk; professional teams have appropriate equipment

## Maintenance Checklist

Beyond cleaning, periodic visual inspection helps catch issues early:

- Visual inspection of panel surfaces (cracks, discolouration, delamination)
- Check for bird nests or debris around panel edges and mounting frames
- Inspect inverter display for error codes or warning lights
- Review monthly energy production against expected benchmarks
- Check visible wiring for signs of damage or rodent activity
- Verify mounting structure bolts and brackets remain secure

Most problems — when caught early — are minor and inexpensive to fix. Left unattended, issues like corroded connections or failed inverter components can cause sustained generation losses.

## Monitoring System Performance

Regular monitoring is often more valuable than cleaning alone. Key habits:

**Check the inverter app or display weekly** — Most modern string inverters and microinverters have companion apps that show daily and monthly generation in kWh.

**Track monthly generation** — Your installer should provide an expected monthly generation estimate at commissioning. Compare actual generation against this baseline each month.

**Investigate significant drops** — A 10–15% sustained drop that isn't explained by weather or seasonal variation warrants a call to your installer.

**Review annual net metering statements** — Your DISCOM's net metering account reflects total export credits. Cross-reference with your inverter data to confirm accuracy.

## When to Call Your Installer

Contact your installer or a service technician if you notice (see our [guide to choosing a solar installer](/learn/how-to-choose-solar-installer.html) for what good after-sales support looks like):
- Inverter offline or displaying fault codes
- Generation consistently below expected by more than 15%
- Visible physical damage to panels (cracks, burn marks, delamination)
- Unusual sounds from the inverter
- Tripped circuit breakers in the solar distribution board

Most quality installers provide annual maintenance contracts (AMC) that include 1–2 inspections per year. An AMC is worth considering for the first 5 years especially, while the system settles in.
