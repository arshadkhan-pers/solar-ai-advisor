## The Core Difference

**On-grid solar** (also called grid-tied solar) connects your solar system directly to the public electricity grid. When your panels generate power, it flows first to your home's loads; any surplus is exported to the grid, and any shortfall is imported from the grid. There are no batteries.

**Off-grid solar** is completely independent of the public electricity grid. All electricity must come from your panels and battery bank. There is no grid backup — if your batteries run out and the sun isn't shining, you have no power.

**Hybrid solar** is a middle ground: grid-connected with battery storage. It provides backup power during grid outages while still enabling net metering and grid interaction.

## On-Grid Solar: The Right Choice for Most Indian Homeowners

For the vast majority of urban and semi-urban Indian households with a functioning grid connection, on-grid is the practical and financially smart choice.

**Advantages:**
- Lowest upfront cost — no batteries needed
- Net metering converts surplus solar into bill credits
- PM Surya Ghar subsidy applies primarily to grid-connected systems
- Simpler installation and maintenance
- Panels last 25+ years; no battery replacement cost

**Limitations:**
- No power during grid outages (unless hybrid is chosen)
- Dependent on DISCOM for net metering approval and credit

**Suitable for:** Any home with a reliable grid connection where daytime grid outages are infrequent.

## Off-Grid Solar: For Remote or Unreliable Grid Locations

Off-grid solar is designed for locations where the grid is unavailable, unreliable, or connection is impractical (remote villages, farmhouses, hill stations, remote commercial sites).

**Advantages:**
- Completely independent of the grid
- Power available even when the grid is down or absent
- No electricity bill from the utility

**Limitations:**
- Significantly higher upfront cost (batteries are expensive)
- Batteries require replacement every 5–10 years depending on chemistry
- System must be sized to cover all loads including worst-case low-sunlight periods
- Cannot claim PM Surya Ghar subsidy (scheme is for grid-connected systems)
- No net metering benefit

**Suitable for:** Locations without grid connectivity, or where grid is available fewer than 8–10 hours per day and the cost of a large battery bank is still better than alternatives.

## Hybrid Solar: Backup Power Without Going Full Off-Grid

Hybrid systems are increasingly popular in cities with frequent power cuts. They give you the best of both worlds: net metering credit during normal operation and battery backup during outages.

**Advantages:**
- Grid-connected with battery backup
- Net metering still available (with caveats — check your DISCOM's rules on hybrid systems)
- Smaller battery bank needed compared to full off-grid (grid is the backstop)
- Can be sized for only the critical loads during outages (smaller, cheaper battery)

**Limitations:**
- Higher cost than pure on-grid (battery + compatible hybrid inverter)
- Battery replacement cost still applies every 5–10 years
- Regulatory clarity on hybrid + net metering varies by DISCOM

**Suitable for:** Urban homes in areas with daily grid outages of 2–6 hours, where backup power for essential loads (fans, lights, fridge) is a priority.

## Side-by-Side Comparison

| Feature | On-Grid | Hybrid | Off-Grid |
|---|---|---|---|
| Upfront cost | Lowest | Medium | Highest |
| Power during outages | No | Yes (limited) | Yes |
| Net metering | Yes | Usually yes | No |
| PM Surya Ghar subsidy | Yes | Yes (grid-connected portion) | No |
| Battery replacement cost | None | Yes | Yes |
| Best suited for | Urban, reliable grid | Urban with power cuts | Remote, no grid |

## How to Decide

**Go on-grid if:** Your grid is reasonably reliable (outages under 2 hours/day), you want the lowest cost system, and your priority is reducing electricity bills through net metering.

**Go hybrid if:** Your area has daily grid outages and you need backup for essential loads, but you still want net metering benefits.

**Go off-grid only if:** Your location genuinely has no reliable grid access, or your grid is so unreliable (8+ hours of daily cuts) that a large battery bank becomes economically justified.

For most Indian homeowners, on-grid is the answer.
